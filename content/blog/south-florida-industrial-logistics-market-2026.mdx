The **South Florida industrial market** is entering a critical inflection point in 2026. After two years of rising vacancy from pandemic-era lows, the tri-county corridor spanning Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties is showing early signs of stabilization — and the fundamentals that made this region the Southeast's premier logistics hub haven't changed. With 247.4 million square feet of total inventory, vacancy settling near 5.7%, and median asking rents above $21 per square foot in Miami, disciplined investors are finding compelling entry points as the construction pipeline clears and tenant demand re-accelerates.

## Tri-County Vacancy and Rent Snapshot

The three counties that make up South Florida's industrial corridor tell distinct stories in 2026, and understanding the divergence is critical for allocating capital.

Miami-Dade remains the anchor. Vacancy has risen from the sub-3% levels of 2023 to approximately 5.7%, with availability (including sublease space) reaching 8.9%. That sounds like softening until you consider context: 5.7% is still well below the national industrial average, and Miami's asking rents have climbed to $20.88 per square foot gross — one of the highest industrial rates in the country. In-place rent growth across the Miami and Tampa metros hit 7.4% over the trailing 12 months as of early 2026, a figure that reflects embedded lease escalations catching up to market.

Broward County is the quiet outperformer. Vacancy has held in the 4.1% to 5.5% range, the tightest among Florida's major metros, while asking rents have pushed to over $17 per square foot NNN, up 2% year-over-year. Broward's advantage is geographic: sandwiched between Miami-Dade and Palm Beach, it serves as the connective tissue for regional distribution and benefits from spillover demand when Miami inventory tightens.

Palm Beach County carries the highest vacancy at 6.3%, with asking rents around $14 per square foot. For value-oriented investors, that discount to Miami (roughly 32% lower rents) and Broward (17% lower) creates an arbitrage opportunity, particularly for tenants being priced out of southern submarkets.

## PortMiami and MIA: The Trade Engine

South Florida's industrial thesis doesn't start with buildings, it starts with infrastructure. PortMiami recorded 1,115,058 TEUs in fiscal year 2025, marking its 11th consecutive year above the one-million-TEU threshold and a 2.35% year-over-year gain. The port's cargo mix skews 46% Latin American and Caribbean, 33% Asian, and 20% European and Mediterranean, making it the undisputed gateway for hemispheric trade.

Miami International Airport compounds the advantage. MIA processed over 3 million tons of cargo in 2024, maintaining its position as the nation's leading airport for international air freight. Together, the port and airport create a logistics gravity well that no competing Florida market can replicate.

The Latin American e-commerce market is projected to surpass $200 billion in 2026, and South Florida sits at the transshipment crossroads. Fulfillment corridors are expanding into secondary markets like Lakeland and Ocala, but the highest-value last-mile nodes remain concentrated in the tri-county area, where 163 industrial properties totaling 17.4 million square feet directly support last-mile operations.

## Small Bay and Last-Mile: The Supply Constraint Story

The most actionable sub-segment in South Florida industrial is small bay warehouse space in the 5,000 to 15,000 square foot range. Tenant demand has decisively shifted toward smaller, more functional spaces, and virtually no new small bay product is being built. The result: small bay properties are projecting 8–12% annual rent appreciation through 2026, outpacing the broader market by a wide margin.

Last-mile delivery costs now consume 53% of total shipping expenses, up from 41% just a few years ago. That cost pressure is driving tenants to secure well-located infill space close to population centers — exactly the type of product that South Florida has in limited supply. With the region adding roughly 170 new residents per day, demand for distribution points serving same-day and next-day delivery windows will only intensify.

About 60% of warehouses nationally are increasing automation budgets by 20% in 2026, implementing robotics and AI-driven systems. For investors, this means tenants are willing to commit to longer leases and invest in tenant improvements — a positive signal for occupancy stability and creditworthiness. ## Construction Pipeline and the Absorption Inflection

The most important macro signal for South Florida industrial in 2026 is the clearing construction pipeline. After years of elevated deliveries that pushed vacancy from historic lows, the development cycle is winding down. National industrial vacancy is expected to peak mid-2026, with absorption improving through the back half of the year and into 2027.

In Miami-Dade, net absorption registered 670,459 square feet in a recent quarter — more than doubling the prior quarter's pace. Tampa posted 840,000 square feet of annual absorption in 2025. The combination of decelerating supply and re-accelerating demand sets up a favorable NOI trajectory for owners who acquired or held through the correction.

For investors with a 3–5 year hold thesis, the current window offers a compelling risk-adjusted entry. Cap rates for prime Miami industrial assets sit in the 5.0–5.3% range, tight by national standards but justified by the rent growth runway and infrastructure moat that South Florida commands. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the current vacancy rate for South Florida industrial real estate?
South Florida industrial vacancy varies by county. Miami-Dade is at approximately 5.7%, Broward County ranges from 4.1% to 5.5% (the tightest in Florida), and Palm Beach County sits at 6.3%. All three remain below or near the national industrial average.

### How much does industrial warehouse space cost in Miami?
Miami industrial asking rents have reached approximately $20.88 per square foot gross, making it one of the most expensive industrial markets in the country. Broward County averages $17.19 per square foot NNN, and Palm Beach offers value at $14.27 per square foot NNN.

### Is South Florida a good market for industrial investment in 2026?
South Florida's industrial market benefits from irreplaceable trade infrastructure (PortMiami and MIA), strong population growth adding 170 residents per day, and a clearing construction pipeline. Cap rates for prime assets range from 5.0% to 5.3%, with in-place rent growth of 7.4% in the Miami-Tampa corridor.

### What is driving warehouse demand in South Florida?
Three primary forces: e-commerce fulfillment (South Florida e-commerce sales grew 18.5% year-over-year), Latin American trade via PortMiami (1.1 million TEUs annually), and last-mile delivery demand driven by population growth. Small bay spaces of 5,000–15,000 square feet are in especially high demand.

### How does Broward County compare to Miami-Dade for industrial investment?
Broward offers the tightest vacancy in Florida (4.1–5.5%) at lower rents than Miami-Dade ($17.19 vs. $20.88 per square foot). Its central location between Miami and Palm Beach makes it a strategic distribution hub with strong spillover demand when Miami inventory tightens.
