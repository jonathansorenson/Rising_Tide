If you are evaluating a commercial property — whether a 200-unit multifamily complex or a single-tenant industrial warehouse — the **NOI calculation** is the first number every investor, lender, and appraiser will ask for. Net Operating Income (NOI) strips a property down to its core earning power: how much cash it generates from operations before debt service, capital expenditures, and income taxes. Understanding how to calculate NOI correctly is the foundation of every underwriting model, every valuation, and every investment decision in commercial real estate.

This guide walks through the NOI formula component by component, applies it to real-world examples across four major asset classes, and provides the operating benchmarks that separate strong investments from underperformers.

## The NOI Formula: Components Explained

The fundamental formula is straightforward:

**NOI = Gross Operating Income − Total Operating Expenses**

But precision matters. The formula expands into a waterfall that starts with a property's top-line revenue and subtracts only the costs directly tied to running the building. Here is the full waterfall:

**Gross Potential Rent (GPR)** represents the total rent a property would collect if every unit or suite were leased at market rates with zero vacancy. GPR is your theoretical ceiling.

**Vacancy and Credit Loss** accounts for the reality that no property operates at 100% occupancy with perfect collections. In 2025, multifamily vacancy nationally averaged roughly 6.5%, while South Florida retail vacancy sat at a historically low 3.5%. The vacancy factor you apply should reflect your specific market and asset class — not a generic assumption.

**Effective Gross Income (EGI)** is GPR minus vacancy and credit loss, plus any other income the property generates. Other income typically includes late fees, pet fees, parking revenue, laundry income, or storage rental. For a well-operated 150-unit multifamily property, other income can add 5–10% on top of base rental revenue.

**Total Operating Expenses** are the costs required to keep the property functioning and income-producing. These include property taxes, insurance, property management fees, repairs and maintenance, utilities, landscaping, marketing, and administrative costs. Critically, operating expenses do *not* include mortgage payments (debt service), capital expenditures, depreciation, or income taxes. Mixing these in is the single most common NOI calculation error.

The result — EGI minus operating expenses — is your Net Operating Income.

## Step-by-Step NOI Calculation Example

Consider a 120-unit multifamily property in Melbourne, Florida — a Space Coast submarket where aerospace-driven employment growth continues to support rental demand.

**Step 1: Establish Gross Potential Rent.** Each unit averages $1,650 per month. GPR = 120 units × $1,650 × 12 months = $2,376,000.

**Step 2: Subtract vacancy and credit loss.** The Brevard County multifamily vacancy rate was approximately 7.2% in Q4 2025, and we'll assume 1% credit loss for non-payment. Vacancy and credit loss = $2,376,000 × 8.2% = $194,832.

**Step 3: Add other income.** Pet fees, late charges, reserved parking, and washer/dryer revenue total $142,000 annually.

**Step 4: Calculate Effective Gross Income.** EGI = $2,376,000 − $194,832 + $142,000 = **$2,323,168**.

**Step 5: Subtract total operating expenses.** Annual operating expenses for this property total $952,499, broken down as follows:

| Expense Category | Annual Amount | % of EGI |
|---|---|---|
| Property Taxes | $261,548 | 11.3% |
| Insurance | $174,238 | 7.5% |
| Property Management (5%) | $116,158 | 5.0% |
| Repairs & Maintenance | $139,390 | 6.0% |
| Utilities | $104,543 | 4.5% |
| Landscaping & Grounds | $46,463 | 2.0% |
| Marketing & Leasing | $34,847 | 1.5% |
| Administrative & Legal | $46,463 | 2.0% |
| Payroll (On-Site Staff) | $28,849 | 1.2% |
| **Total Operating Expenses** | **$952,499** | **41.0%** |

**Step 6: Calculate NOI.** NOI = $2,323,168 − $952,499 = **$1,370,669**.

This produces an operating expense ratio (OER) of 41.0% and an NOI margin of 59.0% both within the healthy range for a stabilized multifamily asset. For context, multifamily OERs typically range from 30% to 45% depending on age, location, and management efficiency.

## Operating Expense Benchmarks by Asset Class

One of the most valuable applications of the NOI calculation is benchmarking. Knowing the typical operating expense ratio for each asset class allows you to identify properties that are either exceptionally well-managed or hiding deferred maintenance and under-reported costs.

| Asset Class | Typical OER Range | NOI Margin Range | Key Cost Drivers |
|---|---|---|---|
| Multifamily | 30–45% | 55–70% | Payroll, insurance, turns |
| Industrial / Warehouse | 15–25% | 75–85% | Minimal if triple-net |
| Retail (Strip/Neighborhood) | 20–35% | 65–80% | CAM, insurance, taxes |
| Office | 35–55% | 45–65% | HVAC, janitorial, TI |

Industrial properties stand out with OERs as low as 15% for triple-net warehouse leases, because tenants bear most operating costs directly. Office buildings sit at the other end of the spectrum, with full-service gross leases pushing OERs above 50% in some markets.

These ranges shifted in 2024–2025 as insurance premiums surged — particularly in Florida and other coastal states — and tariffs on imported construction materials (steel, aluminum, electrical components) drove repair and maintenance costs higher. CBRE reported that average OERs for multifamily assets increased by 1–2 percentage points compared to pre-pandemic levels, a trend that directly compresses NOI if rents do not keep pace.

## What NOI Does NOT Include

This is where experienced operators separate from beginners. The following items are explicitly excluded from NOI, and including them will produce an inaccurate (and often misleading) figure:

**Debt service (mortgage payments).** NOI is a pre-financing metric. It measures property performance independent of how the acquisition is capitalized. Two identical properties can have the same NOI but wildly different debt service obligations depending on their loan-to-value ratios and interest rates.

**Capital expenditures (CapEx).** Roof replacements, HVAC system overhauls, parking lot resurfacing, and unit renovations are not operating expenses — they are capital investments that extend the useful life of the asset. CapEx is accounted for below the NOI line when calculating cash flow after reserves.

**Depreciation and amortization.** These are non-cash accounting entries used for tax purposes and have no place in an NOI calculation.

**Income taxes.** NOI is calculated at the property level, not the entity or investor level. Tax treatment varies based on ownership structure (LLC, LP, REIT, etc.) and individual investor circumstances.

A related metric — **Net Cash Flow (NCF)** — subtracts capital reserves and leasing costs from NOI, giving a more complete picture of the cash available for debt service and distributions. Many lenders use NCF rather than NOI when sizing loans.

## How NOI Drives Property Valuation

NOI is the numerator in the most widely used valuation equation in commercial real estate: the income capitalization approach.

**Property Value = NOI ÷ Cap Rate**

If our Melbourne multifamily example produces $1,370,669 in NOI and comparable properties in the Space Coast trade at a 5.5% cap rate, the implied value is $1,370,669 ÷ 0.055 = **$24.9 million**. ## Common NOI Calculation Mistakes

Even experienced operators make errors that distort NOI. The most frequent include:

**Using asking rents instead of effective rents.** If a property offers two months free on a 12-month lease, the effective rent is roughly 83% of the face rate. NOI calculations must use effective rents, not the headline number.

**Understating insurance costs.** Florida property insurance premiums rose 40–60% between 2022 and 2025 for many coastal CRE properties. Using last year's premium in a forward-looking NOI projection will overstate income.

**Ignoring management fees on owner-operated properties.** Even if you self-manage, the NOI should include a market-rate management fee (typically 3–6% of EGI). Omitting it inflates NOI and makes the property appear more profitable than it would be under third-party management.

**Confusing reserves for CapEx with operating expenses.** Replacement reserve contributions (typically $250–$500 per unit per year for multifamily) are subtracted below the NOI line when calculating NCF, not within operating expenses.

**Not adjusting for lease structure.** A gross lease and a triple-net lease on otherwise identical properties will produce very different OERs. When benchmarking, ensure you are comparing like-for-like lease structures.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is included in a NOI calculation?

NOI includes all rental income, other property income (parking, storage, laundry), minus vacancy and credit loss, minus operating expenses such as property taxes, insurance, management fees, repairs, utilities, and administrative costs. It excludes debt service, capital expenditures, depreciation, and income taxes.

### How do you calculate NOI from a rent roll?

Start by summing all contractual rents from the rent roll to establish gross potential rent. Subtract a vacancy and credit loss factor based on market conditions, add any other income sources, then subtract total annual operating expenses. The result is your Net Operating Income.

### What is a good NOI margin for commercial real estate?

A strong NOI margin depends on asset class. Multifamily properties typically target 55–70%, industrial assets range from 75–85% due to triple-net lease structures, retail falls between 65–80%, and office buildings range from 45–65%. Properties below these ranges may indicate management inefficiency or deferred maintenance.

### Why is debt service excluded from NOI?

Debt service is excluded because NOI measures the property's operating performance independent of its financing structure. Two identical properties can have different debt loads. By excluding debt service, investors can compare properties on a level playing field regardless of how each deal is capitalized.

### How does NOI affect property value?

NOI is the numerator in the income capitalization approach: Property Value = NOI ÷ Cap Rate. At a 5.5% cap rate, every additional $1 of NOI adds roughly $18 to property value. This multiplier effect makes NOI optimization — through rent growth, vacancy reduction, or expense control — the most powerful value-creation lever in CRE.
