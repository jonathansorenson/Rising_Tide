While national industrial vacancy climbed to 7.5% at the close of 2025, **flex warehouse space** on Florida's Space Coast is telling a different story. Brevard County's industrial vacancy sits at just 3.5% (less than half the national figure) and the flex/small-bay segment is where the tightest conditions and strongest tenant demand are converging. For investors tracking Florida's CRE market, the Space Coast flex opportunity is one of the most compelling risk-adjusted plays available in 2026.

## What Makes Flex Warehouse Different — and Why It Matters Now

Flex warehouse, sometimes called flex industrial or small-bay industrial, combines traditional warehouse storage with office, showroom, or light manufacturing components under one roof. These buildings typically range from 5,000 to 50,000 square feet and feature higher parking ratios, dock-high and grade-level doors, and adaptable interior layouts.

The asset class has historically been overlooked by institutional investors chasing large-format logistics deals. That dynamic is shifting. Nationally, small-bay and micro-warehouse vacancy rates remain below 5% in most markets, and only 0.3% of total industrial stock under construction falls in the small-bay category. Supply is structurally constrained, and demand from aerospace, defense-tech, and advanced manufacturing tenants — the exact profile concentrated on the Space Coast — is accelerating.

Two figures stand out. First, the 131,000 square feet of positive net absorption against only 148,000 square feet of pipeline means Brevard is absorbing nearly as fast as developers can build — and construction starts have dropped sharply from 410,000 square feet completed the prior year. Second, cap rates in the 7.2–8.1% range offer meaningful yield premium over coastal Florida metros while fundamentals argue for compression toward 6.2–6.8% by mid-2026.

## Aerospace and Defense: The Demand Engine

The Space Coast's flex market isn't riding a speculative wave. It's underpinned by contractual, mission-critical demand from the aerospace and defense sector.

Blue Origin now operates across 11 sites in Brevard and Orange counties after opening a 750,000-square-foot rocket manufacturing facility on Merritt Island. The company employs nearly 4,000 people locally and has invested over $2.3 billion with 500 Florida-based suppliers. It is also building a new light manufacturing and parts facility in Titusville that will bring roughly 100 additional jobs.

L3Harris Technologies, Brevard County's anchor defense employer, is investing $100 million in a new 94,000-square-foot satellite integration and test facility in Palm Bay. SpaceX continues ramping launch cadence from Kennedy Space Center, with each launch cycle generating downstream demand for component assembly, testing, and warehousing.

Over 15,000 new high-paying positions have been announced by major corporations in the Brevard corridor over the past three years. Each of these positions creates a multiplier effect: defense subcontractors, parts fabricators, and logistics providers all compete for the same limited pool of flex warehouse space. As we discussed in [The Ripple Effect](/blog/the-ripple-effect-space-and-defense-contractors-reshaping-floridas-economy), the economic multiplier from aerospace employment is among the highest of any industry vertical.

## Supply Constraints Favor Existing Owners and Early Movers

New construction in Brevard County has decelerated sharply. The current pipeline of 148,000 square feet is a 64% drop from the 410,000 square feet delivered in the prior 12-month period. The largest project underway — a 100,800-square-foot facility in Titusville — was expected for completion in early 2026, but it arrives into a market that absorbed 131,000 square feet over the same timeframe.

Hines' acquisition of the Titusville Logistics Center for $42.2 million in January signals that institutional capital recognizes the imbalance. Total deal volume across Brevard's industrial sector reached $206 million over the trailing twelve months, with logistics and flex assets accounting for $124 million of that activity. Private investors remain dominant at 64% of buyer composition, while user-buyers represent 22%.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is flex warehouse space in commercial real estate?

Flex warehouse space is a hybrid property type that combines warehouse, office, and sometimes light manufacturing or showroom uses under one roof. Buildings typically range from 5,000 to 50,000 square feet with adaptable layouts, higher parking ratios, and multiple access points. The format is popular with aerospace subcontractors, tech firms, and small manufacturers.

### Why is the Space Coast flex market outperforming the national average?

Brevard County's 3.5% industrial vacancy is less than half the 7.5% national rate because demand is anchored by aerospace and defense employers — SpaceX, Blue Origin, and L3Harris — rather than speculative logistics. These tenants sign long-term leases, invest in buildouts, and create downstream demand for subcontractor space that keeps absorption strong.

### What are cap rates for flex industrial space on the Space Coast?

Industrial cap rates in Brevard County currently range from 7.2% to 8.1%, with forecasts projecting compression to 6.2–6.8% by mid-2026 as institutional capital increases its allocation to the market. This represents a meaningful yield premium over coastal Florida metros where cap rates already sit in the 5.5–6.5% range.

### Is new flex warehouse construction keeping up with demand in Brevard County?

No. The construction pipeline has dropped 64% year-over-year to just 148,000 square feet, while the market absorbed 131,000 square feet over the trailing twelve months. Only 0.3% of national industrial stock under construction falls in the small-bay category, making supply constraints structural rather than cyclical.

### How does aerospace employment affect flex space demand on the Space Coast?

Each major aerospace hire creates multiplier demand for flex space. Blue Origin's 4,000 employees work with 500+ Florida suppliers, many of whom lease flex and small-bay facilities in Brevard County. L3Harris' $100 million satellite facility expansion and SpaceX's increasing launch cadence generate similar downstream demand for component assembly, testing, and warehousing.
